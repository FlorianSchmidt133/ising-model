Model Explainer

The Ising model of population dynamics

A lattice of stochastic units modelling the mouse superficial superior colliculus (sSC): short-range excitation and slow lateral inhibition make sparse, transient blobs of activity emerge. Drive it live below.

01

The idea

Why model the superficial superior colliculus's sparse, blob-like activity with spins?

Two-photon recordings in the mouse superficial superior colliculus (sSC), a midbrain hub that integrates bottom-up visual input with top-down signals, show sparse, transient blob-like patches of co-active neurons that appear, persist and fade, and that shape visual attention. Rather than fit every neuron, we ask whether a simple statistical-physics model, a kinetic Ising model, reproduces the same statistics: how much of the population is active, how clustered it is, and how it responds to a stimulus.

Each lattice site is a unit that is either active (+1) or inactive (−1). Two ingredients shape the dynamics: nearby units excite each other, while a broader surround inhibits with a delay. That competition is what produces transient, localized blobs instead of a frozen, fully-on or fully-off sheet.

This page runs a faithful in-browser port of the model's update rule for teaching. The analysis pipeline itself lives in ising-model/ of the repository (Python, Numba-accelerated).
02

Spins on a lattice

A grid of ±1 units with periodic (wrap-around) edges.

The state is a 2-D grid s(i,j) ∈ {−1, +1}. Each unit corresponds to an imaged tissue voxel; the model grid is 39 × 78, three times the 13 × 26 imaged field, with periodic boundaries so there are no edges. At any moment the fraction of +1 sites is the model's “fraction active”, the quantity we compare to the data.

03

The update rule

Heat-bath (Glauber) dynamics with an excitatory neighbourhood.

We repeatedly pick a random site and re-draw its state. Its local field combines its four nearest neighbours, a slow inhibition field H (next section), and a constant bias:

local = Σ(nearest-neighbour spins) + H(i,j) + bias

The site becomes active with a probability set by the local field and the inverse temperature β (how deterministic vs. noisy the units are):

P( s = +1 ) = 1 / ( 1 + e−2 β · local )

High β ⇒ nearly deterministic (strong fields win); low β ⇒ noisy, near-random. A negative bias keeps the baseline mostly silent, matching the low spontaneous activity in the data.

04

Adaptive lateral inhibition

The ingredient that turns a ferromagnet into transient, localized blobs.

Each site carries a slow field H that integrates the activity of its surround, a diamond-shaped kernel of radius rad that excludes the centre and its four nearest neighbours (so close units only excite, distant ones only inhibit):

inhib = ⟨surround spins⟩ → H ← H − (c · inhib · dt) − (decay · H · dt), dt = 1/(L·M)

When a region lights up, its surround accumulates negative H and is suppressed shortly after, so activity cannot stay put (a non-reciprocal feedback). c sets the inhibition strength, decay how fast H relaxes, and rad the spatial reach. Together they set the size and lifetime of the blobs.

06

From model to data

Fitting the five parameters, and perturbing the fit.

For each condition (Naive, Beginner, Expert, plus No-Spout) the five parameters β, c, decay, rad, bias are screened over a large grid (~25,000 sets) and scored against the recorded dynamics with a Wasserstein distance on the distribution of instantaneous activity and the occurrence of blob-like features. The best-matching simulations define each condition's model; that is Figure 5.

Figure 6 then perturbs the fitted model with two brief bias pulses (at stimulus onset and offset) applied to a localized patch, and measures how strongly activity is amplified, reproducing the state-dependent gain seen across learning. The demos/ folder reproduces both the Expert base model (demo_ising.py) and the exact Figure 6c perturbed trace (demo_figure6c_expert.m).
